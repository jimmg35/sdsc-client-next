## The short version of what's going on in SDSC

Recent SDSC activity has become more outward-facing and more cumulative at the same time. In the last three months, the center has combined new research infrastructure, publications, and invited exchange with a strong showing at AAG 2026, giving SDSC visible momentum across spatial social science, aging, mobility, and public health.

[anchor:sdsc-aag-2026-san-francisco]
The clearest public-facing signal in this window is SDSC's presence at the **2026 American Association of Geographers Annual Meeting** in San Francisco. With **3 faculty members and 7 graduate students** presenting research, plus specialty-group awards and elected board roles, SDSC showed both depth and range. The mix of topics, from clustered regression and local spatial context to older adult mobility, political polarization, and spatial access modeling, reflects a center that is increasingly visible across multiple corners of the discipline.

[anchor:fsu-aws-research-acceleration-fund-american-electorate]
That conference visibility sits alongside a stronger infrastructure story. One of the clearest developments in this window is the **2025-2026 FSU/AWS Research Acceleration Fund award** to Mehak Sachdeva, A. Stewart Fotheringham, and Mason Mathews. With AWS cloud computing support behind a project on the American electorate, SDSC is strengthening the computational backbone needed for large, longitudinal spatial analysis.

[anchor:mehak-sachdeva-fyap-grant-urban-polarization]
That same direction shows up in **Mehak Sachdeva's 2025-2026 First Year Assistant Professor Grant**, which supports the construction of a high-resolution national voter database spanning 2019-2022. Taken together, the two awards show SDSC investing not just in individual studies, but in reusable data and computing capacity for research on political behavior, segregation, and place.

[anchor:amber-dejohn-applied-geography-transit-investment]
At the same time, Amber DeJohn's new *Applied Geography* article on the causal effects of transit investment on neighborhoods adds a publication-level marker of SDSC momentum in mobility and place-based inequality research. The paper connects transportation change to neighborhood outcomes in a way that fits squarely with the center's broader interest in how spatial processes shape social life.

[anchor:goal-symposium-fsu-2026-wrap-up]
Amber's role in hosting the **2026 Geographies of Aging and the Life Course (GOAL) Symposium** at Florida State University broadens that momentum from publication into convening. With Mason Mathews, Weining Kan, Stephen Liwur, Ziqi Li, and Amber DeJohn among the SDSC faculty participants, the symposium signals a more visible interdisciplinary footprint for SDSC in aging-focused scholarship.

[anchor:amber-dejohn-ut-knoxville-digital-health-talk]
Her invited talk at the **University of Tennessee, Knoxville** extends that visibility beyond FSU. Speaking there about digital health in later life places SDSC research into a wider public health conversation and shows the center's work traveling through both formal publication channels and invited academic exchange.
